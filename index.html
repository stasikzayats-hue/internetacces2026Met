01 — Mechanisms

How Governments
Censor the Internet

Internet censorship isn't a single switch — it's a layered system of technical, legal, and social controls working together.

🔒
IP Blocking
Governments instruct ISPs to block traffic to specific IP addresses. All Chinese internet traffic must pass through state-controlled gateways overseen by the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology. [1]
🔍
Deep Packet Inspection
DPI technology — like Russia's TSPU boxes — inspects packets in real-time, enabling Roskomnadzor to throttle or block any service without ISP cooperation, and to detect VPN protocols like VLESS. [4]
🌐
DNS Manipulation
The domain name system is poisoned so requests for blocked sites fail silently or redirect to government-approved pages — invisible to most users.
⚖️
Legal Pressure & AI Rules
China now requires AI-generated content to embody "core socialist values," holding LLM providers liable as publishers. Russia criminalizes calling the war a "war" — up to 15 years in prison. [1]
👁️
Mass Surveillance
China uses facial recognition, Wi-Fi sniffers, and IMSI catchers. All mobile users must complete face scans to register for services. Russia's SORM system stores years of communications for FSB access on demand. [1][4]
📵
Allowlist Shutdowns
Rather than blocking specific sites, governments cut all global internet access and permit only a whitelist of approved services. Russia keeps 57 state-approved services live; Iran adopted this permanently after January 2026. [3][4]

02 — Case Study
🇨🇳
China
The Great Firewall

China operates the world's most sophisticated internet censorship system — the "Great Firewall" (防火长城) — and has held the title of most restrictive internet environment for over a decade. [1] As of December 2023, China has 1.09 billion internet users, representing 77.5% of the population, with 99.9% accessing via mobile devices.

Despite high speeds (135.71 Mbps mobile, 223.57 Mbps fixed-line), the Firewall makes foreign content "sluggish" due to intensive filtering. The fastest connections are in Shanghai; the slowest and most heavily censored are in the Xinjiang Uyghur Autonomous Region. Approximately 317 million people remain offline, with 51.8% in rural areas. [1]

"The internet under Chinese censorship is not a broken internet — it is a different internet, built to serve the Party." — Freedom on the Net 2024 [1]
Human Cost (2023–2024) [1]

Sun Lin, an activist who filmed anti-Xi protests, died from injuries sustained during a November 2023 police raid. Filmmaker Chen Pinlin was detained for posting a "White Paper" protest documentary — up to five years in prison. A Tibetan monk received 18 months for sharing a Dalai Lama audio clip in a WeChat group.

New Self-Media Rules (July 2023) [1]

Accounts with over 500,000 followers must display real names. Live streamers giving medical, legal, or financial advice must hold professional qualifications. Content from Large Language Models must embody "core socialist values" — with providers held responsible as publishers under CAC censorship rules.

VPN Crackdowns & Targeted Control [1]

In 2023, a programmer was fined 1.1 million yuan (approx. $150,000 CAD) and had earnings confiscated for using a VPN to work for an overseas company. Activists and their families face "targeted disconnections" of home internet ahead of politically sensitive dates. All mobile users must pass face scans to register for services.

03 — Case Study
🇮🇷
Iran
The Allowlist State

Iran has moved beyond temporary shutdowns to a permanent, identity-linked "allowlist" model — a new phase of digital authoritarianism. In January 2026, in response to widespread protests, Iranian authorities enacted a near-total information blackout lasting ten days (January 8–18, 2026). [3]

This was achieved not through simple routing changes but through sophisticated filtering at the network edge and higher layers of the protocol stack. When connectivity returned, it was not restored to its previous state — Iran adopted a permanent allowlist where the vast majority of the global internet remains blocked by default. International access is now a "tightly monitored privilege." [3]

"A pilot program requires users to undergo in-person identity verification, register fixed IP addresses, and sign pledges against misuse." — Freedom on the Net 2025 / arXiv research [3]
The January 2026 Total Shutdown [3]

For ten days, Iran was essentially severed from the global internet using sophisticated protocol-stack filtering. During this period, users turned to illegal Starlink terminals and peer-to-peer apps like Bitchat and Noghteha — which operate over Bluetooth and local Wi-Fi, completely bypassing standard landline infrastructure.

The New Allowlist Normal [3]

Post-shutdown, approved platforms (Google, Gmail, ChatGPT) remain accessible for basic functions, while Instagram, Telegram, WhatsApp, and X stay blocked. The model treats internet access as a surveilled privilege — mirroring China's approach — rather than a universal service.

Tor Survived [3]

OONI (Open Observatory of Network Interference) tests after the January 2026 shutdown confirmed that Tor remained unfiltered — a testament to the ongoing cat-and-mouse struggle where even Iran's aggressive filtering cannot maintain total, consistent control.

04 — Case Study
🇷🇺
Russia
The Sovereign Internet

Russia has rapidly constructed a sophisticated "sovereign" internet built on deep packet inspection, strategic shutdowns, and state-mandated surveillance. The foundation is the TSPU system — specialized filtering hardware installed at every ISP but controlled exclusively by state agency Roskomnadzor. [4]

During shutdowns — officially justified by drone-attack threats — Russia employs a whitelist model: only 57 state-approved services remain functional (including RIA Novosti, major banks, and the state messenger Max), while the rest of the global internet is cut off. [4]

"Russia's SORM system mandates that telecoms store years of communications metadata and content for the FSB to access on demand." — Mediazona, 2026 [4]
TSPU & Deep Packet Inspection [4]

TSPU boxes analyze traffic signatures in real time, letting Roskomnadzor throttle or block any service — YouTube, WhatsApp, foreign messengers — without ISP cooperation. As of April 2026, Russian internet companies must block access to their own platforms for any user detected using a VPN.

Max: The State Surveillance Messenger [4]

To fill the void left by throttled WhatsApp and Telegram, Russia aggressively promotes "Max," a national messenger that critics and technical analysts warn is designed for FSB surveillance and lacks end-to-end encryption.

The VPN Offensive (2026) [4]

Russia has blocked advanced protocols like VLESS and pressured Apple to remove hundreds of VPN apps from the Russian App Store. The SORM surveillance system stores all communications metadata for state access on demand — creating total legal and technical control.

05 — Resistance

When the Wall
Comes Down

Despite the sophistication of these systems, censorship has repeatedly failed — through technical evasion, public resistance, or political collapse. These cases prove the wall is not impenetrable.

📡
Russia's 2018 Telegram Failure [3]
The Kremlin's first attempt to block Telegram failed spectacularly. Telegram used rapidly cycling IP addresses and distributed servers across major international cloud providers. Roskomnadzor ended up blacklisting millions of unrelated IPs — briefly breaking smart water boilers and other utilities — before abandoning the effort entirely.
💻
China's Green Dam & GitHub Reversals [3]
In 2009, a mandate to install "Green Dam Youth Escort" filtering software on all Chinese computers failed after massive public opposition. Similarly, China's 2013 block on GitHub was reversed following widespread complaints from the domestic software development community — both times, economic and technical interests defeated censorship.
✊
Bangladesh's 2024 Uprising [3]
During August 2024 student-led protests, the government restricted mobile internet for 11 days and blocked social media. These measures failed to stop the mobilization. The repressive leadership was ultimately ousted — leading to a significant improvement in Bangladesh's internet freedom status. Shutdowns can fail politically.
🛰️
Iran 2026: Starlink & Mesh Networks [3]
During Iran's January 2026 shutdown, users turned to illegal Starlink terminals and peer-to-peer messaging apps like Bitchat and Noghteha — which operate over Bluetooth and local Wi-Fi, bypassing all standard landline filtering entirely. No internet infrastructure needed.
🔐
Snowflake & Meek: Cloud Disguise [3]
Technologies like Snowflake and Meek (used by Tor) disguise censored traffic as normal sessions with major cloud services like Microsoft Azure — which states are reluctant to block for economic reasons. Even China's machine-learning Great Firewall struggles to block these reliably.
🧅
Tor's Survival in Iran (2026) [3]
OONI tests after Iran's January 2026 shutdown confirmed that Tor remained unfiltered — demonstrating the ongoing cat-and-mouse struggle where even the most aggressive state censorship cannot maintain consistent total control. The resistance is technical, distributed, and global.
06 — Upstanders

People Who
Fought Back

Behind every fight for internet freedom are individuals who refused to stay silent. These upstanders — from education activists to whistleblowers — each faced injustice and used their strengths to create real change. Research based on the Canadian Museum for Human Rights "Be an Upstander" project. [5]

📚
Malala Yousafzai
Education Activist
Injustice
Cultural oppression — girls were denied education and faced lack of knowledge and basic human rights.
Strength
Courage and public speaking — she spoke out openly despite direct threats to her safety.
Change
Advocated globally for equal access to education; her activism contributed to real policy changes and inspired millions worldwide.
⚖️
Viola Desmond
Civil Rights Pioneer
Injustice
Racial segregation — denied equal access to public spaces and treated as a second-class citizen in her own country.
Strength
Determination and legal courage — she refused to accept an unjust law and formally challenged it in court.
Change
Her case challenged racial segregation in Canada and inspired civil rights action across the country.
📰
Roman (Journalist)
Independent Reporter
Injustice
People did not have fair access to news and accurate information — state-controlled media silenced truth.
Strength
Journalism skills, public speaking, and willingness to report with fairness and kindness.
Change
Provided access to independent information, helping communities make informed decisions and hold power accountable.
🌐
Tim Berners-Lee
Inventor of the Web
Injustice
Information was hard to access and share — knowledge was siloed and inaccessible to most of humanity.
Strength
Technical knowledge — he invented the World Wide Web and deliberately kept it open, free, and non-proprietary.
Change
Invented the web to make information universally accessible — the foundation of everything this website discusses. He continues to advocate for a free and open internet.
🔎
Edward Snowden
Whistleblower
Injustice
Lack of transparency in government — the public was unaware of massive secret surveillance programs violating their privacy.
Strength
Willingness to take the risk and put the truth before personal safety — sacrificing his career and freedom.
Change
Exposed surveillance programs and sparked global debates about privacy, government accountability, and digital rights that continue today.

08 — Response

What You Can Do

Internet freedom is a global issue — even if you live in a free country, the battle for an open web affects everyone. Be an upstander.

01 —
Stay Informed
Follow Freedom House, Access Now, NetBlocks, OONI, and the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) for up-to-date reporting on internet freedom worldwide.
02 —
Support Circumvention Tools
The Tor Project, Psiphon, Snowflake, and Meek build free tools helping people in censored countries access the open internet. Donate or contribute to their development.
03 —
Advocate for Digital Rights
Contact your elected representatives about internet freedom policy, trade agreements with censoring governments, and export controls on DPI surveillance technology.
04 —
Practice Good Digital Hygiene
Use encrypted communication (Signal), a trusted VPN, and strong passwords. Normalizing privacy tools makes them less suspicious for people in repressive countries.
05 —
Hold Tech Companies Accountable
Demand transparency from platforms about government data requests and content removals. Call out companies that hand over user data to authoritarian regimes.
06 —
Be an Upstander
Like Malala, Tim Berners-Lee, Viola Desmond, and Edward Snowden — find where your strengths can create change. In your school, your community, or your country. Injustice anywhere is a threat to freedom everywhere.
