01 — Mechanisms

How Governments
Censor the Internet

Internet censorship isn't a single switch — it's a layered system of technical, legal, and social controls working together.

🔒
IP Blocking
Governments instruct ISPs to block traffic to specific IP addresses, making entire services unreachable at the network level.
🔍
Deep Packet Inspection
DPI technology inspects the contents of internet packets in real-time, allowing filtering of specific content even within otherwise allowed sites.
🌐
DNS Manipulation
The domain name system is poisoned so that requests for blocked sites either fail or are silently redirected to government-approved pages.
⚖️
Legal Pressure
Laws compel platforms to remove content, hand over user data, or store data locally — or face bans, fines, or criminal charges for staff.
👁️
Mass Surveillance
Citizens are monitored in real-time. Knowing you are watched creates a chilling effect — self-censorship becomes as powerful as direct censorship.
📵
Internet Shutdowns
During protests or crises, governments order entire regions or nationwide internet blackouts, cutting citizens off from communication and information.

02 — Case Study
🇨🇳
China
The Great Firewall

China operates the world's most sophisticated internet censorship system — known as the "Great Firewall" (防火长城). Built over two decades, it combines technical controls, legal coercion, and an army of human censors to create an entirely parallel internet ecosystem under state control.

Rather than simply blocking content, China has built domestic alternatives to every major Western platform. These platforms are required by law to censor politically sensitive content, report users who violate speech rules, and store all data on servers accessible to the government.

"The internet under Chinese censorship is not a broken internet — it is a different internet, built to serve the Party." — Digital Rights researchers
The Golden Shield Project

China's nationwide surveillance and censorship infrastructure spans ISPs, mobile networks, and all major tech companies. It employs AI to auto-detect and remove sensitive content within seconds of posting.

VPN Crackdowns

While VPNs are widely used, China periodically tightens enforcement — particularly before sensitive dates like the Tiananmen anniversary. Individuals have been fined and detained for VPN use.

Keyword Filtering

Millions of keywords trigger automatic deletion across platforms like WeChat and Weibo. Censored terms include political leaders' names in critical contexts, protest-related words, and even historical events.

03 — Case Study
🇮🇷
Iran
The National Intranet

Iran has built one of the world's most restrictive internet environments, tightened dramatically since the 2022 Mahsa Amini protests. The government maintains a "national intranet" — a domestic network designed to eventually function independently of the global internet.

During the 2022 protests, mobile internet was shut down repeatedly. Instagram and WhatsApp — two of the last remaining popular Western platforms — were blocked. The government openly discussed permanently severing from the global internet.

"Iran's response to the 2022 uprising was a blueprint for digital authoritarianism: cut the internet, then watch the revolution starve." — Access Now, Digital Rights organization
The SHOMA Network

Iran's "National Information Network" (SHOMA) provides government-controlled domestic services — email, cloud storage, banking — designed to make the global internet unnecessary for daily life.

Throttling as a Weapon

Iran frequently throttles (slows down) internet speeds to make services unusable without fully blocking them, giving authorities deniability while achieving the same effect.

The Cyberpolice (FATA)

Iran's dedicated internet police force arrests citizens for social media posts, pursuing dissidents, journalists, and activists who criticize the government online — even from abroad.

04 — Case Study
🇷🇺
Russia
The Sovereign Internet

Russia is not yet at China or Iran's level of censorship — but since the 2022 invasion of Ukraine, it has been moving fast. The "Sovereign Internet Law" (RuNet), passed in 2019, gave the government infrastructure to disconnect Russia from the global internet entirely.

When war broke out in 2022, Russia blocked dozens of independent news outlets, Facebook, Instagram (labelling Meta an "extremist organization"), and restricted Twitter. Calling the war a "war" — rather than a "special military operation" — became a criminal offence online.

"Russia isn't building a Great Firewall from scratch — it's using the war to accelerate a long-planned disconnection from the free web." — NetBlocks, Internet monitoring group
The RuNet Law (2019)

Russia's "sovereign internet" law requires all ISPs to install government-controlled filtering hardware (TSPU) capable of rerouting or cutting off all Russian internet traffic — creating an internal network the Kremlin can wall off on command.

War-Time Censorship (2022–)

Spreading "false information" about the Russian military carries up to 15 years in prison. Over 1,000 websites were blocked in the first weeks of the war. VPN usage surged 2,000% overnight.

Platform Coercion

Russia pressured platforms for years to remove content and store Russian user data locally. When they refused, Twitter was throttled (then later unblocked), Telegram was temporarily banned, and LinkedIn has remained blocked since 2016.

06 — Response

What You Can Do

Internet freedom is a global issue — even if you live in a free country, the battle for an open web affects everyone.

01 —
Stay Informed
Follow Freedom House, Access Now, NetBlocks, and the Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) for up-to-date reporting on internet freedom worldwide.
02 —
Support Circumvention Tools
Organizations like Tor Project and Psiphon build free tools that help people in censored countries access the open internet. Donate or contribute to their development.
03 —
Advocate for Digital Rights
Contact your elected representatives about internet freedom policy, trade agreements with censoring governments, and export controls on surveillance technology.
04 —
Practice Good Digital Hygiene
Use encrypted communication (Signal), a trusted VPN, and strong passwords. Normalizing privacy tools makes them less suspicious for people in repressive countries.
05 —
Hold Tech Companies Accountable
Demand transparency from platforms about government requests, content removals, and data sharing. Support companies that publish transparency reports and resist government overreach.
06 —
Amplify Silenced Voices
Share work from journalists, activists, and civil society organizations in censored countries. Giving their work international attention makes it harder to silence.
